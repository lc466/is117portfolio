About

About me

I’m developing practical skills in UI design, web prototyping, lightweight research, and digital product thinking—while working toward a Junior Frontend Developer role.

My workflow starts with structure: semantic HTML, a dependable layout system, and typography that scales. Then I refine spacing, interaction details, and responsive behavior using DevTools and feedback from instructors and peers.

I’m transparent about what I’m still learning—especially deeper JavaScript patterns and performance tuning—but I stay consistent with fundamentals like meaningful headings, keyboard-friendly navigation, and contrast checks.

Strengths

  • Structured execution: layouts become repeatable patterns before polishing visuals.
  • Collaborative mindset: I revise quickly when critique makes the UI clearer.
  • User-aware fundamentals: readable type, responsive behavior, and basic accessibility checks.

AI in learning: I use AI to clarify concepts and explore alternatives—then I rewrite explanations in my own words and validate changes directly in the browser.

Focus

Professional direction

Junior Frontend Developer: implementation-first UI work that respects maintainability and accessibility—scoped honestly for where I am today.

Not my focus (yet): large-scale systems architecture—I'm prioritizing strong frontend fundamentals first.

AI workflow · intentional collaboration

AI supported this portfolio where it accelerates thinking—but testing, editing, and ownership stayed mine.

1

Career research

Used AI to compare junior frontend expectations and decode jargon—then verified details against coursework, MDN-style references, and postings I reviewed myself.

2

Portfolio planning

Asked for outline options (sections, accessibility reminders, realistic scopes). I chose what matched my timeline and removed anything that overstated experience.

3

Writing & revision

Draft support for clarity and tone—followed by manual edits so claims match what my repos can prove.

4

Visual direction

Explored style keywords and UI motifs as inspiration—final decisions followed implementation constraints and contrast/accessibility checks.

5

Coding support

Used AI to compare approaches and explain errors—then validated layouts and interactions in the browser with DevTools.

6

Review discipline

When suggestions looked fine visually but failed semantics, responsiveness, or keyboard UX—I rewrote them, even if it took longer.

Why AI didn’t replace my judgment

Tools can suggest—but only I can take responsibility for scope honesty, originality, accessibility tradeoffs, and shipped quality. That accountability is what makes a student portfolio credible.
